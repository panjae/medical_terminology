Chapter 13. I. OVERVIEW

Smooth Muscle: Structure and Function

🔬 Definition & Overview

Smooth muscle is an involuntary muscle type responsible for adjusting the luminal diameter of hollow structures. While skeletal muscle is designed to contract rapidly for activities such as running, jumping, and throwing under voluntary control, smooth muscle performs innumerable involuntary activities ranging from rapid responses like controlling pupil diameter to sustained contractions lasting an hour or more such as the internal anal sphincter. These varied demands have necessitated many organ-specific adaptations to its structure and function.

💪 Skeletal Muscle

  • Voluntary control (somatic nervous system)
  • Rapid contraction - running, jumping, throwing
  • Neurotransmitter: ACh only
  • Somatic nerve terminal activation
  • Regular sarcomere arrangement
  • High myosin concentration

🌊 Smooth Muscle

  • Involuntary control (autonomic nervous system)
  • Slow but sustained contraction
  • Multiple stimuli: chemical, electrical, mechanical
  • Hundreds of ligands modulation
  • Irregular minisarcomere arrangement
  • Low myosin but similar actin levels

💡 Core Concept

The most remarkable features of smooth muscle are its functional diversity and adaptability. While using the same basic principles as skeletal muscle (actin-myosin interactions), smooth muscle has evolved organ-specific adaptations. Contraction begins when intracellular Ca²⁺ concentrations rise, and force is generated when myosin thick filament head groups bind to and pull on actin thin filaments. However, unlike skeletal muscle, the Ca²⁺-sensitive step has been transferred from the thin filament (via troponin) to the thick filament (via myosin phosphorylation), which is the defining characteristic of smooth muscle contraction.
